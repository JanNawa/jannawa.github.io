Why a system, not a funnel

A funnel chart tells you volume dropped somewhere. It doesn't tell you whether the drop happened because the ad copy was wrong, the lead scoring model was miscalibrated, the CRM had the wrong contact, or the deal was stuck behind a compliance review nobody was watching. Treating Spend → MQL → SQL → Opportunity → Closed Won → Revenue as one continuous, instrumented pipeline.

The core idea: Every handoff in this pipeline is a place where data quality decays (e.g. wrong contact, a stale usage number, an unscored lead.) A GTM System is to instrument each handoff so the decay is visible immediately, not three months later when CAC:LTV shows up broken in a board deck.

The revenue engine

Optimizing each handoff

Every arrow above is a place volume or quality leaks. Here's the diagnosis-to-fix pattern for each one, at a usage-based B2B company where the buyer, the champion, and the person who signed up for the free sandbox are often three different people.

HandoffCommon leakGTM engineering fix
Spend → MQLHigh traffic, low form fills. Tracking gaps or form frictionVerify GTM/tag firing first; shorten forms dynamically once an enrichment API identifies company size from the business email domain
MQL → SQLVolume is up but Sales rejects most leads as poor fit or unreachableReweight the lead-scoring model toward high-intent product signals (API docs viewed, sandbox key generated); block free-mail domains at signup
SQL → OpportunityThe listed contact isn't the actual buyer or technical championRun contact-role enrichment before routing. Map the plan tier and use case to the title that actually owns it, not the most senior name found
Opportunity → Closed WonDeals stall in security review or API evaluationAutomated triggers: SOC2/compliance packet after 5 days stuck; Slack ping to a Solutions Engineer if the blocker is technical
Closed Won → RevenueSigned customer never ramps usage to the contracted volumeReverse ETL from Revenue data back to CRM so CS sees real usage, not just contract value, and can intervene before the first renewal
Revenue → CAC:LTVBlended CAC hides which channel × plan-tier combinations are actually unprofitableCohort CAC:LTV by channel and tier, not just overall. Reallocate spend away from combinations below the 3:1 bar

Three of these, in more depth — the ones with the most engineering surface area

MQL → SQL

Score intent, not just fit

A downloaded whitepaper and a generated sandbox API key are not the same signal. Require the latter or a pricing-page visit before a lead crosses into SQL, regardless of firmographic score.

SQL → Opportunity

Route to the buyer, not the name on file

The CEO signs nothing at a mid-market usage-based buyer. Map plan tier and use case to the actual title — Head of Payments Ops, Platform Engineering Lead before an SDR ever reaches out.

Opportunity → Closed Won

Watch the stall, not just the stage

Days-in-stage is a better leading indicator than stage itself. A deal sitting in "Security Review" past its median duration needs an automated nudge before it needs a discount.

The sourcing & enrichment engine

The same pipeline pattern that sources and qualifies outbound targets also keeps the SQL → Opportunity handoff honest: don't hand Sales a name, hand them the right name, with a confidence score attached.

Pipeline architecture

01
Signal ingestion

Every signal lands in one normalized place

Product usage, firmographic and tech-stack signals, and intent data (job postings, review sites) all flow into a normalization job that standardizes company and domain records before anything downstream touches them.

02
Company & contact research

Two tiers, cheapest source first

Tier 1 is LinkedIn, for org structure and reporting lines. Tier 2 is the open web, careers pages and press. For anything LinkedIn doesn't surface. Enrichment APIs (Apollo/Clay) fill in remaining firmographic gaps.

03
Buyer-role mapping

Map the decision, not the seniority

A contact-selection step matches plan tier and use case to the title that actually owns the decision.

04
Confidence & ICP scoring

Score before anything gets sent

Every contact gets an H/M/L confidence score based on LinkedIn match, domain match, and email-pattern validation. Only High pushes to CRM automatically; Medium and Low sit in a human review queue.

05
Suppression, dedup & sync

Upsert, never re-create

Company names are normalized and clustered by domain before insertion, then synced to CRM with custom fields, usage percentile, expansion-signal flag, plan-tier fit, and handed off to outbound automation.

Failure modes & how they're handled

IssueSolution
Hallucinated or stale contactsOnly accept a contact with a LinkedIn match, domain match, and email-pattern validation all agreeing
Duplicate companies across signal sourcesNormalize names and cluster by domain and address before any CRM insert/upsert, never re-create
Usage signals go stale between batchesTimestamp every enrichment record and tie the refresh cadence to the warehouse's own batch job, not a fixed schedule

Where AI fits, and where it doesn't: useful for idea generation, first-pass research, and the repetitive work of drafting enrichment queries across hundreds of accounts. Not trusted for the final send/no-send call on a contact, or for judging whether a borderline account actually fits the ICP. Unambiguous stays a human decision.

Signal-based outbound, in practice: a customer crossing 80% of their monthly API-call plan limit fires an expansion-signal flag straight into CRM. The same mechanism that would flag a newly expanded account as an upsell trigger, not just a churn-risk watch.
